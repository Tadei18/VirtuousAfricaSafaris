National parks are the headline names. But some of the best wildlife
experiences in Kenya happen next door, on **community conservancies** — and
there's both a practical and an ethical reason we lean into them.

## The practical case

Conservancies set their own rules, and they tend to be better ones:

- **Vehicle limits.** Far fewer cars at a sighting than in the public reserve.
- **Off-road tracking.** Guides can follow a hunting cheetah instead of watching
  it disappear.
- **Night drives and walking safaris.** Both are banned in national parks and
  both are unforgettable.

## The ethical case

Most Kenyan wildlife lives outside fenced parks, on land communities could
otherwise fence and farm. Conservancies pay landowners a lease for keeping it
open to wildlife — which means **your visit becomes a direct financial reason to
protect lions, elephants and rhino.**

It's tourism doing what it should: making the living animal worth more than the
dead one. When you travel with us, a share of every booking flows into that
system — and we're happy to show you exactly where.
